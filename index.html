§01 — The Moment

First World Cup played mostly on US soil since 1994.

Forty-eight teams. Sixteen cities. Three nations. One Final at MetLife on a Sunday afternoon in July. The 2026 FIFA World Cup is the biggest single sporting event ever attempted on this continent — and the United States is hosting 78 of the 104 matches, including every match from the quarterfinals on.

The USMNT plays all three group-stage matches at home venues: SoFi twice, Lumen Field once. That hasn't happened since Bill Clinton was in office and the Macarena hadn't broken yet.

This guide is the version of the World Cup you can hand a friend who doesn't follow soccer and a friend who watches every Champions League midweek. Same site, both readers.

§03 — The Shape

Pochettino's USMNT, on a single page.

Base shape: 4-2-3-1. When the opponent invites pressure: 4-3-3. When we need to shut a match down: 3-4-3. Same principles in every shape: dominate the ball, win it back in six seconds, finish the ninety with more sprints than them.

  • Attack
  • Midfield / Defense
  • Goalkeeper
  • Press triggers

01 · High line, high press

Don't sit. The forward line presses the center-backs; the midfield squeezes the gaps; the back four steps to the halfway line. We make the field smaller on every restart.

02 · Six-second rule

The instant we lose the ball, we have six seconds to win it back. After six, we drop into shape and start again. Counter-pressing isn't a tactic — it's a clock.

03 · 35 meters of compactness

The distance from our deepest defender to our highest attacker stays inside 35 meters. Closer than that and we're cramped. Farther and we're broken in two.

04 · Pulisic free

Captain plays where the game asks. Nominal LW; in reality he drifts to the half-spaces, the channels, even the right when defenders track. The team moves to him.

05 · Vertical, then patient

First look is forward. Always. If forward isn't on, we recycle through Adams, reset the angle, and look forward again. We don't pass for the sake of possession.

06 · Set pieces are 30%

Roughly a third of World Cup goals come from dead balls. Richards on attacking corners, Ream as the back-post screen, Pulisic over the inswinger. We rehearse them like a play call.

§03b — The Three Questions

What actually decides the USMNT's June.

  1. How does Pochettino manage Tyler Adams's load?

    Quad cleared in April, back in Bournemouth's XI — the question is no longer fitness, it's minutes. The structure pivots on him. Cardoso and Tessmann can play; neither is Tyler. Without an anchor, the press has no spine.

  2. Does Pulisic break the drought before kickoff?

    Sixteen Serie A games without scoring is the longest barren run of his career. A scoring captain in the opener changes the tournament. A silent one becomes a problem we'll solve, not wait out. Watch his first 15 minutes vs Paraguay.

  3. Is Dest at full ninety on June 12?

    Back from the hamstring on May 3 — 30 minutes vs Ajax. Availability isn't the question; durability is. If he can do the full 90, Weah pushes high and the front three is symmetric. If not, Weah drops to right back and we lose a winger. The team is structurally different.

§05 — The Three Matches

Calm. Test. Festival.

Match 1: open clean, win it. Match 2: weather the storm. Match 3: a packed stadium and a real opponent. Here's what to watch — and a score guess we'll be held to.

The Calm

The vibe

Tournament opener. The whole country watching. Paraguay is the wildcard — they snuck through CONMEBOL on grit, not glamour. Their game is defensive solidity plus counter-attack: exactly the matchup that exposes a possession side that isn't sharp on day one.

US backline against Paraguay's transition speed. If the US wins the first 50/50 in midfield clean, the whole group opens up. If Paraguay gets two corners in the first twenty minutes, settle in.

— Between matches

The soul of the thing.

A USMNT World Cup is more than 11 men on a pitch. It's the chant in section 134, the rivalry that won't die, and the three goals every American soccer fan can describe with their eyes closed.

01

The American Outlaws

Supporters' group · founded Lincoln, NE · 2007

A sea of stars-and-stripes scarves and one massive banner that reads "I BELIEVE THAT WE WILL WIN" — that's them. Eighty-plus chapters across the country, tens of thousands of members, the only supporters' group with a proper march to the stadium before every USMNT home match. They invented the modern soundtrack of US soccer fandom: "I-I-I-I believe…"

If you can be at SoFi or Lumen, walk with the Outlaws. Find your local chapter, show up two hours before kickoff, march in singing.

02

Dos a Cero

The score · the chant · the rivalry

For twelve years and seven consecutive qualifying matches, the USMNT beat Mexico 2-0 at home. Almost always in Columbus, Ohio. Cold, sleet, the old Crew Stadium, El Tri's worst nightmare. The Mexican fans coined the score's name in mockery: "Dos a Cero." US fans took it back as the chant.

The streak ended in 2016 — and not a single American soccer fan has stopped saying it. If you ever beat Mexico in this tournament — at SoFi, in a knockout, in a Final — the chant returns within ninety seconds.

03

The Pantheon

The three goals · 2010 · 2014 · 2022

  • Donovan vs Algeria, 91' — June 23, 2010, Pretoria. Stoppage-time winner that took the US to the Round of 16. Ian Darke's call — "Oh, can you believe this?!" — is part of the audio canon now.
  • Dempsey vs Ghana, 1' — June 16, 2014, Natal. Thirty-two seconds in. Run, cut, finish. The fastest US goal in World Cup history. The country lost its mind before settling in for an entire match.
  • Pulisic vs Iran, 38' — November 29, 2022, Doha. The captain hurt himself scoring the only goal that mattered. Played the half. Came off in pain. The country exhaled.

2026 is the cycle that wants to add the fourth one.

04

The 1994 Effect

Hosting plants a sport · 1994 → MLS '96 → 2026 → ?

The 1994 World Cup was the price the country paid for getting a domestic professional league: FIFA awarded the tournament on the condition the US would launch MLS. Two years later, ten teams kicked off in San Jose, Tampa, Columbus. A whole generation of American soccer was born from that bargain.

2026 is the second host moment. What does it plant? A second tier of professional women's leagues? A youth-development renaissance built on the 26-man roster's path? A USL promotion-relegation conversation that finally stops being theoretical? Watch what gets announced before July 19.

05

Pochettino, on the staff

The most-credentialed coach US Soccer has ever hired

Espanyol → Southampton → Tottenham → Paris Saint-Germain → Chelsea → here, since September 2024. Mauricio Pochettino is the first manager to have run a UEFA Champions League knockout campaign before standing on a USMNT touchline. U.S. Soccer is paying for that pedigree because the host country can't afford to look outclassed.

His Argentine identity is its own subplot: in 2026, the host nation's coach grew up where Messi grew up, in the same league system that produced the 2022 World Cup champion. Pochettino isn't a hire. He's a statement.

06

The Hershey Kid Comes Home

Christian Pulisic · b. Sept 18, 1998 · Hershey, PA

The captain grew up in Hershey, Pennsylvania, on a soccer field that was three blocks from the chocolate factory. He was the first American teenager taken seriously as a Champions League prospect since Tab Ramos. He's now 27, captain, has a Champions League title, and is playing his first World Cup in his own country. The story arc that begins with a kid kicking a ball outside a chocolate factory and ends at MetLife on July 19 is the whole sport's myth in a single life.

§07 — The Shirt

A jersey lineage, told in ten kits.

From Where's Waldo (1994) to Be the Light (2020) to the wavy stripes Nike just dropped for 2026 — the story of US soccer told through what the team wore on its back.

94
1994

Where's Waldo

The home shirt of the USA-hosted '94 World Cup — red and blue stars on a stonewashed denim background. A divisive, gloriously mid-90s kit that has aged into a cult classic. You either wore one in Pasadena or you wished you had.

95
1995

The Sash

One red sash diagonally across a white shirt. Worn at Copa America '95 — the run that beat Argentina 3-0 and put US soccer on the map for an entire generation. Every minimalist jersey designer since has been chasing this template.

06
2006

The Denim Away

Faded blue, white sash returning, tonal stars across the chest. Worn through the Germany 2006 group stage. It has aged better than the campaign — most pundits call it the most beautiful USMNT shirt ever made.

12
2012

Centennial Hoops

Red and white horizontal hoops. A nod to the 1916 USMNT shirt and a hard pivot away from white-with-trim. Klinsmann era, polarizing on day one, beloved by year three.

14
2014

Don't Tread on Me

The Gadsden-flag-coiled-snake hidden in a tonal pattern across the away shirt at Brazil '14. A risky political reference rendered subtle enough to slide by — and the shirt the team wore the night Tim Howard saved everything.

16
2016

Bomb Pop

Red, white, and blue vertical bands for Copa America Centenario. Looked like a popsicle. Played like one. Beloved precisely because it was unserious — and because the team made the semifinals in it.

20
2020

Be the Light

The most experimental USMNT shirt of the modern era — a celestial, almost iridescent design with hidden constellation patterns. Marked a real departure from "white with red trim" and the moment Nike started treating this kit like a serious design brief.

22
2022

Qatar Whites

The Qatar 2022 home kit — clean white with a subtle wordmark texture, tonal red trim. The shirt that beat Iran. The shirt the kids who watched on a couch in Cincinnati will frame in twenty years.

26
2026 · Home

The Wave

Unveiled March 16, 2026. Curvy red-and-white stripes — a deliberate riff on the beloved 2012 Centennial Hoops, but warped into motion. Designed in consultation with McKennie, Adams, and Weah over two years. It's loud. It will divide opinion for a week and own every American highlight reel for a summer.

The Stripes, but moving.

26
2026 · Away

The Stars

The quiet sibling. Rich navy base, with stars embellished in subtle vertical lines — tonal, almost hidden until the light catches them. The opposite design philosophy from the home shirt: where The Wave shouts, The Stars whisper. The shirt the players will want to wear. The shirt critics will rank #1 by August.

One stars, one stripes — both at the World Cup.

Why this matters. Nike + U.S. Soccer designed a single kit set worn by all 27 U.S. Soccer teams — including the USWNT — for the first time at a World Cup. A USMNT jersey is the only soccer shirt most American casuals own; they buy a Nike kit, not a club shirt. 2026's, played at home, will be the most-worn USMNT shirt in history. Get one early — both, if you can swing it.

§08 — Glossary

For the friend you're bringing to their first match.

Everything they'll hear on the broadcast, in plain English. Bookmark this and send it to the people who said yes to the watch party.

Pitch
The field. Don't say "field" if you can help it. The broadcast will say pitch.
Kit
The full uniform — shirt, shorts, socks. "Home kit" = white. "Away kit" = darker / blue.
Match
Not "game." 90 minutes plus stoppage time, two halves, a 15-minute break.
Stoppage time
Extra minutes at the end of each half to make up for time lost to subs, fouls, injuries. The ref decides how much. The clock never stops during play — that's why scores still change at "92'."
The press
When a team chases the ball aggressively in the opponent's half — what Pochettino's USMNT does all match. The opposite is "sit deep."
The 6 / The 8 / The 10
Midfield positions, not jersey numbers. The 6 sits in front of the back four (Adams). The 8 box-to-box (McKennie). The 10 plays under the striker, runs the attack (Tillman).
Fullback
The defender on the left or right edge of the back four. They overlap the wingers and sprint more in 90 minutes than any other position. Antonee Robinson runs harder than your treadmill goes.
Center back
The two defenders in the middle of the back four. Tall, mean, strong in the air. Richards and Ream for the USA.
The 9
The center forward. The striker. Folarin Balogun. Touches the ball less than anyone else on the team and changes the entire match when he does.
Set piece
A free kick, corner, or throw-in. Roughly 30% of World Cup goals come from set pieces. Don't get up to grab a beer when one starts.
Offside
A goal-scorer can't be ahead of the second-to-last defender at the moment a teammate passes them the ball. If the broadcast cuts to a freeze-frame with a yellow line on the screen — that's an offside review. Just trust the line.
VAR
Video Assistant Referee. The booth that double-checks goals, penalties, and red cards. It's slow. It's frustrating. It usually gets the call right.
Yellow card
A formal warning. Two yellows in one match = a red. Two yellows across the group stage = suspended for the next match. This is why every quarterfinal has at least one star sitting out.
Red card
You're out. Your team plays the rest of the match a player down. You also miss the next match. Don't earn one in the World Cup, ever.
Brace / Hat-trick
Two goals (brace) or three goals (hat-trick) in one match. Pulisic with a brace at SoFi will be the day's first push notification on every American phone.
Nutmeg
Pushing the ball through your defender's legs and collecting it on the other side. The most disrespectful move in the sport. The crowd loses its mind.
Group of Death
The hardest group in the bracket. Three traditional powers + one tough wildcard. Group D (USMNT) is not the Group of Death. Group K (whatever it ends up being) usually is.
The Round of 32
New for 2026. Top 2 from each of 12 groups (24) + 8 best 3rd-place finishers = 32. Then it's straight knockouts: 32 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1.
Penalties / Shootout
If a knockout match is tied after 90 + 30 minutes of extra time, you settle it with five penalty kicks each. Then sudden death. The most stressful three minutes in sport.
American Outlaws
The official US soccer supporters' group, founded in Lincoln, Nebraska in 2007. Eighty-plus chapters, thousands of members, the people in the stars-and-stripes scarves singing "I Believe That We Will Win." If you're at a USMNT match and you want to know where the noise is, walk toward the Outlaws.
Dos a Cero
"Two to nothing" in Spanish — the score the USMNT beat Mexico by, seven straight times in qualifying from 2001 to 2013, almost always in Columbus, Ohio. Mexican fans named the streak in mockery; US fans took it back as a chant. The biggest rivalry in CONCACAF lives inside this score.
Cap
One appearance for the senior national team. Pulisic has 80+ caps. Tim Howard had 121. They're the currency of national-team careers — the broadcast will say "earning his 12th cap tonight."
Friendly
An international match that doesn't count toward a tournament. Used for tactical experiments and friend-of-the-program players. The USMNT plays send-off friendlies in late May before this World Cup.
"I Believe That We Will Win"
The American Outlaws chant. Starts slow, builds, ends in a roar. You'll hear it before kickoff and again every time the US scores. "I-I-I-I believe / I believe that / I believe that we / I believe that we will win!"
"Up the stars, up the stripes"
The unofficial closing chant. You'll hear it. You'll join in. Don't overthink it.
§09 — Dark Horses

The teams that ruin brackets.

Argentina is Argentina, France is France, Spain is Spain. These are the squads that win you a pool you weren't supposed to win.

01

Morocco

Africa · semifinalists in 2022

Walid Regragui's defensive shape is still one of the most disciplined in international football. Hakimi at right back (racing back from a thigh injury suffered in the UCL semis), Ziyech still pulling strings, Brahim Díaz creating — the same spine that eliminated Spain and Portugal in Qatar. Don't pick against them in the round of 32.

02

Uruguay

South America · Bielsa

Marcelo Bielsa coaches like he's behind in points and needs a goal in the last minute, every match. Darwin Núñez has barely played competitively at Al-Hilal since February — but a fit Núñez running channels for 90 is unsustainable for any back four. The bracket-buster you bet on, not the one you fear.

03

Japan

Asia · won qualifying easily

The most technically gifted Japan side ever assembled. Kubo, Mitoma, Kamada in attack; Endo as the calmest #6 outside Europe. They beat Germany and Spain in 2022. They are better now. Quarterfinal floor.

04

Croatia

Europe · the Modrić farewell

Modrić's last World Cup. Croatia made the semis in '18, the semis in '22, and they will not stop until somebody beats them. The squad is older — and still nobody plays possession in the final third more cleanly under pressure.

05

Colombia

South America · finalist energy

Lost the 2024 Copa America final to Argentina and they want it back. Luis Díaz is a nightmare on the left. James Rodríguez can still bend a ball through eight defenders. The dark horse with the highest ceiling on this list.

06

Senegal

Africa · the depth chart

The deepest African squad in the tournament. Sadio Mané is no longer the headline — and that's a feature, not a bug. Pape Matar Sarr in midfield, Iliman Ndiaye creating, Édouard Mendy in goal. Will sneak into a quarterfinal.

Longer shots, in one line each.

  • Ecuador — physical, organized, beat the host in the 2022 opener.
  • Mexico — host bump + a generational U-23 group.
  • Switzerland — quietly made the round of 16 in '06, '14, '18, '22. The most underrated team in the tournament every cycle.
  • Belgium — last great cycle for the 2018 generation, with Doku and Lukaku still firing.
  • Iran — Sardar Azmoun + Mehdi Taremi is a strike pair from a top-six European side.
  • USA (yes, you) — the host's bracket is the friendliest possible on paper. Don't underrate the bounce.
§10 — Predictions

Things we'll be wrong about.

Bracket calls, breakout players, and the one prediction we'll be held accountable to in August. Hold us to all of them; we'll buy the first round.

P1

The USMNT advances to the quarterfinals at minimum.

That's a step beyond the 2022 Round-of-16 exit, and it's the floor of what this squad can do at home with this draw. If they make the semis, ESPN re-runs the 1994 Rossi-Romario clips for a week and the country falls back in love with the sport. We'd take that bet at modest odds.

P2

The Final: Argentina vs Spain

Messi's last World Cup, possibly. Yamal's first deep run. The two best collective attacking sides on the planet. We'll take Argentina 2-1 in extra time at MetLife — but we won't be sad if Spain gets it.

P3

Golden Boot: Lamine Yamal

Eighteen years old. The best player in La Liga even with a hamstring strain that ended his club season — and he's expected back in time for Spain's opener. Tournament structure gives him five matches of left-footed cuts onto his right inside the box. Six goals is the floor.

P4

Breakout USMNT player: Diego Luna

Comes off the bench at 65', changes the press's tempo, scores once and assists twice in 7 group-stage substitute appearances total. By the round of 16 he's starting.

P5

Pulisic, on a knee at SoFi.

The viral moment of the tournament: the captain scores a knockout-stage winner in his own country, drops to one knee, eyes closed, head down — the shot the broadcast holds for nine full seconds. The country exhales on every TV at the same instant. The TikTok of a six-year-old in an Outlaws scarf screaming next to her dad. This is the moment people show their grandkids.

P6

The biggest upset will come in the Round of 32.

The new round, before anyone has settled into the bracket. A top-eight seed loses to a third-place qualifier nobody had in their pool. Bookmark this — we're calling it Belgium.

P7

The single best fan experience of the tournament: Mexico City opener.

The Azteca, June 11, 80,000 in green, the host nation kicking off the world's biggest sporting event. Get to Mexico City for it if you can possibly afford to.

P8

By July 20, US youth soccer enrollment doubles.

The 1994 effect. The 1999 Women's World Cup effect. After a tournament played at home with a deep run, every kid who watched a USMNT match wants to be Pulisic. Soccer becomes the #2 youth sport in America by Christmas.

Send this page to the friend who says soccer is boring. Read the glossary together. Pick a host city. Plan the trip. We'll be wrong about half of this — but the World Cup is better when you watch it with somebody who cares.
